The letterpress technique

The letterpress technique originated in the 15th century. The term "letterpress" comes from the technique in which metal and wood blocks of type are literally pressed into the paper on impression. There has recently been a resurgence in letterpress prinring. The technique, which stands apart from digital practices is now seen as the pinnacle of customization and quality in printing.

While letterpress printing relies on the same practices it did at its inception, new techniques have emerged. The use of polymer plates is a relatively new practice which Illumination Letterpress uses. Instead of wood or metal plates, a more flexible and durable plate is made from resin. These can be made much quicker and affordable than the wood or metal relics that are now found in museums and antique stores.

Some say letterpress takes a "bite" into the paper which the impression is left on. It creates a tactile impression which has been lost in the offset and digital printing age. The heavy paper stock is also something unique to the process. Thick, heavy, soft paper is not easily printed through other printers, if it can be at all. With letterpress, it is required to showcase the impression.

Letterpress's come in a variety of sizes, from small table top presses to large scale ones, similar to the press we have. Our press is large half-ton cast-iron platen press. It was made by the Challenge-Gordon company in 1897 and still runs just as great as it did when it came off the factory line.

The Process

Initial Concept

The process begins with drawing out the initial concept. This is usually derived from a quick meeting with client to go over a few ideas and directions. Here we have a drawing for a boy's birth announcement. The client was looking for a formal announcement that also kept in mind the playful excitement of a newborn baby

Design Finalization

After the proofing process is copleted and the client has approved a layout the design is converted to solid black and white separations. For this design two separations were made, one for blue and one for orange. Thee separations are now ready to be sent away for plate making.

Polymer Plates

Horray the plates have arrived! We have out plates made from polymer plates, a highly flexible, durable and strong resin which allows for a deep and precise impression. Years ago these plates would have been made out of metal or wood, this relatively new process allows for much easier use and creative flexibility. We have all of our plates created by the leading polymer plate manufacturer, Boxcar Press.

Printing

Now it's time for the best part, printing. Registration marks are carefully measeured and pins are set to keep everything in place. Many test runs are done to ensure the pressure and inking is even throughout. Once everything is inked and ready to go paper is fed to the press one at a time. For this birh announcement we would run the paper through once for the blue and once for the orange.
